Part 3

Finding Common Clinical Answers in CS 3D Imaging

Practical navigation patterns for general dentistry cases. Each card is independent, so a dentist can jump straight to the clinical question.

Odontogenic sinus lesion

  1. Open MPR and orient the axial slice through the maxillary sinus floor.
  2. Follow the involved tooth roots in sagittal and coronal views.
  3. Look for loss of sinus floor cortication, periapical rarefaction, mucosal thickening adjacent to the apex, or a visible tract from tooth to sinus.
  4. Use thin slices and adjust contrast before concluding that a sinus finding is isolated from the dentition.

Internal vs external resorption

  1. Center the tooth in MPR, then scroll axially from crown to apex.
  2. Internal resorption tends to remain centered in the canal space as the canal path is followed.
  3. External resorption usually begins on the root surface and may spare the canal until advanced.
  4. Confirm in sagittal and coronal views before assigning prognosis.

External cervical resorption

  1. Set a small field review around the cervical third of the tooth.
  2. Scroll axial slices at the CEJ and slightly apical to it.
  3. Map the portal of entry, circumferential spread, pulpal proximity, and bone communication.
  4. Document restorability and whether surgical access is buccal, lingual, mesial, or distal.

Suspected crack or vertical root fracture

  1. Reduce slice thickness and inspect the root in axial slices.
  2. Do not rely only on seeing a fracture line, especially near posts or dense fillings.
  3. Look for a narrow, isolated periodontal defect, J-shaped or halo bone loss, or localized buccal or lingual cortical breakout.
  4. Correlate with probing, bite testing, transillumination, and the 2D image.

Missed canal or complex morphology

  1. Start in axial view at the chamber floor and move apically in small increments.
  2. Use canal tracking from the pulp chamber rather than searching randomly in the 3D volume.
  3. Watch for sudden canal merging, C-shaped anatomy, MB2 anatomy, or calcified pathways.
  4. Use the crosshair to keep the suspected canal synchronized across all three views.

Implant site planning

  1. Trace the arch and open the curved slicing view.
  2. Confirm ridge width, height, concavities, sinus floor, nasal floor, and nerve position.
  3. Measure in the cross-sectional view, not on a panoramic reconstruction alone.
  4. Keep the proposed implant inside the available restorative envelope.

Inferior alveolar nerve tracing

  1. Use curved slicing to align the mandibular arch before tracing.
  2. Identify the mandibular canal in cross-sectional slices, then confirm it on adjacent slices.
  3. Check the mental foramen exit and anterior loop region before measuring implant clearance.
  4. Record the nearest anatomy and the slice where the measurement was made.

TMJ screening findings

  1. View both condyles in corrected sagittal and coronal orientation.
  2. Compare right and left cortical outline, flattening, osteophytes, erosions, and joint space symmetry.
  3. Remember CBCT shows hard tissue well but does not show the disc position directly.
  4. Refer when symptoms and osseous findings suggest pathology beyond routine dental management.

Incidental pathology review

  1. Systematically review the entire captured volume, not only the tooth or implant site.
  2. Check sinuses, nasal cavity, mandibular canal, cortical borders, condyles, and airway space.
  3. Separate dental findings from nonodontogenic findings in your notes.
  4. Refer to an oral and maxillofacial radiologist when a finding is outside your interpretive comfort.

Periodontal defects and furcations

  1. Orient the tooth long axis vertically in the sagittal and coronal views.
  2. Scroll through buccal, lingual, mesial, and distal surfaces instead of relying on one slice.
  3. Look for crater depth, furcation involvement, dehiscence, fenestration, and relationship to endodontic lesions.
  4. Confirm periodontal findings clinically because thin bone can disappear from partial volume artifact.

Surgical extraction risk

  1. Use cross-sectional slices to evaluate root shape, divergence, ankylosis signs, sinus proximity, and nerve proximity.
  2. For mandibular third molars, trace the canal and inspect whether the cortical canal boundary is interrupted.
  3. For maxillary posterior teeth, evaluate sinus floor relationship and septa.
  4. Use the 3D rendering only as an orientation aid; make measurements in MPR views.

Endodontic working anatomy map

  1. Center the tooth, then align the long axis in all three planes.
  2. Map canal entrances, curvature, calcification, apical anatomy, and proximity to cortical plates.
  3. Use axial scrolling to find missed anatomy, then verify the path in sagittal and coronal views.
  4. Export key screenshots for the chart or referral conversation.

Part 4

Artifact Pattern Guide

A quick visual logic guide for deciding whether the finding is likely artifact, anatomy, or disease.

Movement artifact comes first because it is widely recognized as the leading cause of artifacts in dental CBCT scans. Patient motion can create duplicated borders, blur fine anatomy, and make small defects look larger or smaller than they are. Before diagnosing subtle fracture, resorption, dehiscence, or cortical interruption, first ask whether the whole volume shows motion.

Same CBCT machine, different patients: the comparison below isolates patient movement as the practical difference dentists can look for when image quality changes from scan to scan.
