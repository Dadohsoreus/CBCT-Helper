Part 3

Finding Common Clinical Answers in CS 3D Imaging

Practical navigation patterns for general dentistry cases. These are intentionally broad enough to use across scanners, patients, and clinical questions.

Start every review the same way

  1. Use MPR first, not the 3D rendering, for image review and measurements.
  2. Orient the area of interest so the tooth or ridge is straight in at least two planes.
  3. Scroll slowly through adjacent slices before deciding whether a finding is real.
  4. Save screenshots that show the finding in more than one plane.

Tooth-to-sinus questions

  1. Follow the root apices, sinus floor, and adjacent mucosa in sagittal and coronal views.
  2. Look for a dental source plus a matching sinus response, not mucosal thickening alone.
  3. Ask whether the cortical sinus floor is intact, thinned, or interrupted.
  4. When the relationship is uncertain, describe the uncertainty rather than overcalling origin.

Resorption mapping

  1. Center the tooth and scroll from crown to apex in axial slices.
  2. Decide whether the defect starts inside the canal space or on the external root surface.
  3. Describe location, depth, pulpal proximity, and bone communication.
  4. Use the scan as an anatomic map, then confirm significance with clinical findings.

Crack and fracture confidence

  1. Do not require a visible crack line; it may be hidden by voxel size or artifact.
  2. Look for supporting bone patterns: isolated narrow defects, halo loss, or buccal/lingual breakout.
  3. Be cautious near posts, crowns, and root filling material.
  4. Interpret CBCT together with probing, bite testing, symptoms, and 2D images before any clinical decision.

Endodontic anatomy

  1. Start at the chamber floor and follow canal paths apically in axial slices.
  2. Use the synchronized crosshair to confirm the same structure in all three views.
  3. Map missed anatomy, curvature, calcification, perforation risk, and apical anatomy.
  4. Keep the scan question specific and document what clinical uncertainty the image is helping clarify.

Implants and surgical risk

  1. Trace the arch and evaluate cross-sectional slices at the proposed site.
  2. Measure ridge width, height, concavities, sinus or nasal floor, and nerve position in cross-section.
  3. Keep the restorative goal, anatomy, and patient-specific risk factors in the same view.
  4. Record the slice and plane used for key measurements.

Periodontal and bony defects

  1. Evaluate buccal, lingual, mesial, and distal surfaces separately.
  2. Differentiate periodontal defects from endodontic lesions by tracking their origin and extent.
  3. Thin bone may disappear because of artifact or resolution limits, so avoid overcalling dehiscence.
  4. Use CBCT as an anatomic map, then confirm with probing and clinical attachment findings.

Whole-volume responsibility

  1. Review beyond the immediate tooth: sinuses, nasal cavity, canals, cortices, condyles, and airway space.
  2. Separate dental findings from incidental or nonodontogenic findings in the chart.
  3. Refer when a finding is outside the clinical question or your interpretive comfort.
  4. Document what was reviewed and why the scan was taken.

Part 4

Artifact Pattern Guide

A quick visual logic guide for deciding whether the finding is likely artifact, anatomy, or disease.

Movement artifact comes first because it is widely recognized as the leading cause of artifacts in dental CBCT scans. Patient motion can create duplicated borders, blur fine anatomy, and make small defects look larger or smaller than they are. Before diagnosing subtle fracture, resorption, dehiscence, or cortical interruption, first ask whether the whole volume shows motion.

These figures are used as endodontic literature context, not broad CBCT-use rates.

Impact on endodontic diagnosis. The provided study "The impact of cone beam computed tomography (CBCT) on the choice of endodontic diagnosis" reports diagnosis changes for at least one tooth in 41% of patients and 35% of evaluated teeth.
